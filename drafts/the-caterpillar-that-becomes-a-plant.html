The Caterpillar That Becomes a Plant

It doesn't, of course — no plant is anywhere in the story. But the fungus that hollows out a caterpillar and sprouts a stalk from its head has convinced people for six centuries that it's half-animal, half-vegetable, and made itself worth more than gold along the way. Here's the biology, the gold rush on the roof of the world — and the reputation the whole price is really built on.

Every spring, tens of thousands of people go down on their hands and knees in the alpine meadows of the Tibetan Plateau and crawl, faces inches from the ground, hunting for something the size of a matchstick. What they're looking for is a dead caterpillar with a thin dark stalk growing out of its head — a two-part object that is half insect and, to the naked eye, half twig. The old Tibetan name for it, six hundred years old, is yartsa gunbu: "summer grass, winter worm." The Chinese name, dōng chóng xià cǎo, says the same thing in the other order — winter worm, summer grass. Both names describe a creature that seems to be a caterpillar in the cold months and a plant in the warm ones. It is neither. It's one of the strangest fungi on Earth, and gram for gram it has become more valuable than gold. Established

Winter worm, summer grass

Start with the thing the name gets wrong, because getting it right is the whole delight. Nothing here becomes a plant. The "summer grass" is a fungus — Ophiocordyceps sinensis, a relative of the mind-snatching Ophiocordyceps we've met before in this bestiary, and an entomopathogen: a fungus that makes its living by killing insects. Established Its victim is the larva of a ghost moth (family Hepialidae), a fat caterpillar that lives underground in the plateau's turf, high up between about 3,000 and 5,000 metres, feeding on roots in the dark. Established

Sometime in late summer or autumn, fungal spores meet a caterpillar and germinate inside its living body. The fungus then does what its cousins do: it grows through the insect, kills it, and slowly converts the entire caterpillar into a rigid, fungus-packed mummy — a hard shell holding a mass of fungal tissue in the exact shape of the animal it ate. The dead larva stays put, head up, just below the surface, through the winter. Then, when the ground warms, a single slender fruiting body — a dark stalk a few centimetres long — pushes up out of the caterpillar's head and breaks the surface of the meadow to release its spores. Established A worm all winter; a "blade of grass" in summer. The folk name is a careful, honest description of what you actually see — it just reaches for the wrong kingdom to explain it.

One footnote for the record-keepers, because it's the same move we've made before with the mind-snatcher: until 2007 this fungus was called Cordyceps sinensis, and you'll still see that name everywhere, especially on labels. A molecular reshuffling of the group that year moved it into the genus Ophiocordyceps. Same fungus, better-placed on the family tree. Established

Himalayan gold

Now the part that turns a parasitized caterpillar into a headline. This fungus is one of the most valuable biological commodities on the planet — traded under the nickname "Himalayan gold," and the nickname undersells it. Top-grade caterpillar fungus has sold for more than a hundred thousand US dollars per kilogram; one widely reported 2017 figure out of Beijing put the best material above $140,000 a kilo, which by weight was more than three times the price of actual gold. Established Prices climbed staggeringly through the 2000s — roughly a 900% rise on the Tibetan Plateau between 1998 and 2008 — and the biggest, most intact specimens command the steepest premiums. Established

Every gram of it is gathered by hand. There is no machine for this and no shortcut: someone has to be on the ground, eyes down, spotting a dark thread against alpine soil. That has made the fungus the economic center of gravity for an enormous stretch of high country. For many rural households on the plateau it is the single largest source of cash income in the year — for some families, reporting suggests, the great majority of what they earn — and it makes up a meaningful slice of the local economy in the harvest districts of Tibet, Qinghai, Sichuan, Yunnan, Nepal, and Bhutan. Established A season on your knees in the mud can be worth more than a year of any other work available up there.

Top-quality caterpillar fungus has traded for more than US$100,000 per kilogram — by weight, several times the price of gold — making it one of the most valuable biological commodities on Earth. Established

A gold rush with a body count

A wild thing worth more than gold, gathered by hand from a commons, goes exactly how you'd expect. The species is now listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List, and in China it has been treated as endangered. Established A 2018 study in PNAS laid the decline at the feet of two forces working together: relentless overharvesting, and a warming climate shrinking the cold high-elevation conditions the fungus needs. Yields that collectors describe from a generation ago — a hundred pieces in a day — have fallen to a fraction of that. Established

The money has drawn blood, too. Control of the good meadows during the few weeks of harvest has sparked deadly fights: a group of farmers was murdered in a dispute over collecting grounds in northern Nepal in 2009, with villagers later convicted; the trade became lucrative enough to matter to both sides during Nepal's civil war. Established And there's a quieter hazard in the product itself — wild-harvested fruiting bodies have been found carrying high levels of arsenic and other heavy metals, enough that China's market regulator moved to control their sale in 2016. Established

What the gold is really built on

So why does a small dead caterpillar cost more than a diamond of the same weight? Not for its flavor, and not because it's pretty. The entire towering price rests on a single thing: a reputation. For roughly six centuries the caterpillar fungus has been prized in Tibetan medicine — the oldest known written mention is from the late 1400s, by the Tibetan physician Zurkhar Nyamnyi Dorje, who described it as an aphrodisiac — and it entered the Chinese pharmacopeia by 1694. It has been sold ever since as a tonic: for vigor, stamina, the lungs, the kidneys, the libido. Folklore / marketing

Here is where we plant our flag and are careful, because this is a wonder piece, not a health piece. We can tell you, as a matter of documented culture and economics, that this is what the fungus is believed to do and marketed to do — that belief is a real, load-bearing historical fact, and it's the reason the whole trade exists. What we are not going to do is tell you it works, because that's a different and much harder question, and the honest answer today is that the science doesn't settle it. As one plain summary of the evidence puts it, the caterpillar fungus "is marketed for various health benefits but lacks sufficient scientific evidence for safety or effectiveness." Folklore / marketing Whether any of the tonic claims hold up under real trials is exactly the kind of question our grounding pillar exists for — and it's one we'll take on properly, on its own, another day. For now: the reputation is genuine, ancient, and enormously valuable. Its truth is unproven. Both of those things are true at once.

Two grounding notes worth having in your pocket the next time you pass a jar of "cordyceps" capsules for $40. First: the compound the marketing usually leans on, cordycepin, is really the calling card of a different, cheaper, farm-grown relative, Cordyceps militaris — wild Ophiocordyceps sinensis has very little of it, and one 2017 study of the biosynthetic machinery concluded it makes none at all. Established Second: almost nothing sold as "cordyceps" is the wild worm-and-grass at all. The genuine article resisted farming for decades — you can grow the fungus's mycelium in a vat or on grain, but coaxing the full fruiting body out of an infected caterpillar at scale is a very recent and limited feat. So the powders and capsules on the shelf are overwhelmingly grain-grown mycelium, or an entirely different substitute species, wearing the famous name. Established The thing itself never left the mountain.

Where we get honest

This one sits right on the line we're most careful about, so let's say plainly where the edge is. The biology in this piece — the ghost-moth caterpillar, the mummification, the stalk from the head, the price, the gold rush, the conservation crisis — is solid, sourced ground. The part we are deliberately not adjudicating is whether the caterpillar fungus actually does anything for a human body. It has been used as a tonic for six hundred years, and it is sold that way today for real money; those are facts about culture and commerce, and we've reported them as exactly that — not as evidence that it works. The published assessments say the evidence for safety or effectiveness isn't there yet, and we're not going to leap over that gap with wonder. "People have believed X for centuries" and "X is true" are two different sentences, and keeping them apart is the whole job.

And the two facts that best capture the gap: the signature "active compound" is barely present in the real species, and the real species is barely present in the market. When the most-cited molecule turns out to belong to the cheaper cousin, and the jar on the shelf turns out to be grain-grown mycelium of something else entirely, you're looking less at a proven medicine than at one of the great stories the human appetite for a cure has ever told itself. The fungus is genuinely astonishing. That's more than enough to be worth writing about — and it's a good reminder that "astonishing" and "medicinal" are claims on different rungs of the ladder.

How we labeled it

  • Established The caterpillar fungus is a fungus, not a plant: Ophiocordyceps sinensis infects, kills, and mummifies underground ghost-moth larvae at 3,000–5,000 m, then grows a stalk-like fruiting body out of the dead caterpillar's head. (Reclassified from Cordyceps sinensis in 2007.)
  • Established It is one of the most valuable biological commodities on Earth — "Himalayan gold" — with top grades trading above US$100,000/kg (several times the price of gold by weight), hand-collected, and a leading source of cash income for many plateau households.
  • Established It is IUCN Vulnerable and declining under combined overharvesting and climate change; the trade has driven deadly conflict, and wild material can carry high arsenic/heavy-metal levels (regulated in China since 2016).
  • Folklore / marketing Its entire value rests on a ~600-year-old reputation as a tonic/aphrodisiac in Tibetan and Chinese medicine. We report that reputation as documented culture and commerce — not as evidence of effect. Published assessments say it "lacks sufficient scientific evidence for safety or effectiveness." Whether it works is a grounding-pillar question we're deferring, not answering here.
  • Established Cordycepin, the compound most invoked in "cordyceps" marketing, is the signature of the cheaper cultivated relative Cordyceps militaris; wild O. sinensis has little to none. Most products sold as "cordyceps" are grain-grown mycelium or substitute species, not the wild worm-and-grass.
