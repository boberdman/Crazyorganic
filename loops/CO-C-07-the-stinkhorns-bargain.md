## Stage 1 — Topic Brief (Research)

**Pillar:** 1 — The unexplored kingdom (wonder)
**Working title:** The Stinkhorn's Bargain *(kept as written in the backlog — no de-hyping needed; the "bargain" framing doesn't overclaim anything contested)*
**The big question / hook:** Most mushrooms let the wind scatter their spores for free. Stinkhorns fired the wind — and spend real energy building a smell engineered, molecule by molecule, to mimic a rotting corpse, inside a body that can unfurl faster than almost anything else alive, so that flies will do the delivery instead.

**Key points:**
1. Stinkhorns (family Phallaceae, ~21 genera / 77 species, worldwide, concentrated in the tropics) begin life as an underground "egg" containing the entire pre-built fruiting body; the mature form can erupt within an hour. [established]
2. The unfurling is powered by water absorption expanding pre-packed, folded tissue ("cellular/reverse origami"), not new cell growth — measured at up to 5 mm/minute in *Phallus indusiatus*; separately, *P. impudicus* clocked at 10–15 cm/hour (egg to mature in 1–2 days) and can exert ~1.3 kPa of pressure, enough to crack asphalt. [established]
3. Instead of wind dispersal, stinkhorns smear spores into a sticky gleba and pay the metabolic cost of an elaborate stench to recruit flies, which carry spores on their bodies and in their gut to fresh substrate. [established]
4. The odor is chemically engineered convergent mimicry of both carrion (sulfur compounds — methanethiol, hydrogen sulfide, dimethyl sulfide, dimethyl trisulfide) and feces (phenol, indole, p-cresol); a 2010 study found the same chemistry independently evolved in fly-pollinated "carrion flowers." [established]
5. The odor's compound count changes across the fungus's life — quiet in the egg, loudest (dozens of compounds) at maturity, fading once slime is carried off. [established]
6. A named field study (Schremmer 1963) documented which insects respond: blow-flies feed and defecate spore-dense droppings soon after leaving; beetles visit but mostly eat fungal tissue rather than move spores. [established]
7. Get-it-right/diversity beat: the family's ~21 genera converge on the same chemical trick from very different body plans — *Phallus* (simple stalk+cap), *Clathrus* (latticed cage — lattice stinkhorn, octopus stinkhorn), *Aseroë* (red starburst disc), *Mutinus* (slender dog stinkhorns). [established]
8. History/culture beat: Aldrovandi's 1560 description, Gerard's 1597 "pricke mushroom," Linnaeus's 1753 naming (*impudicus* = "shameless"); Gwen Raverat's 1952 memoir describing her aunt Henrietta "Etty" Darwin (Charles Darwin's daughter) hunting and burning stinkhorns near Cambridge. [established, documented cultural anecdote]
9. Honesty flag: *P. impudicus* fruiting bodies cluster near badger sett entrances (24–39 m) — a real, measured spatial pattern (Sleeman 1995/1996). The appealing causal story ("this protects the badger colony from disease by drawing carrion-clearing blow-flies") is the researchers' proposed interpretation, not a demonstrated mechanism. Hold the clustering as established and the protective-benefit narrative as preliminary/interpretive.

**Claims-risk flag:** none for §4 hard lines (no psilocybin, no cultivation instruction, no health/medical claim about the fungus). One item deliberately excluded: Wikipedia's Phallaceae page notes a related species (*P. indusiatus*) contains "pharmaceutical compounds with potential for cancer treatment" (Habtemariam 2019) — cut entirely rather than hedge, since it sits needlessly close to the §4 #3 health-claim line and isn't central to the story. The dimethyl-trisulfide/fungating-wound aside that *was* kept is a chemistry parallel (shared compound, shared odor), not a claim about the fungus's medical use — reviewed at QA and judged not to cross the line. Foraging/edibility content (eggs are edible raw) also deliberately omitted to stay clear of any instructional framing.
