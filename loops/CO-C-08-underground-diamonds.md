## Stage 1 — Topic Brief (Research)

**Pillar:** 1 — The unexplored kingdom (wonder)
**Working title:** Underground Diamonds *(kept as written in the backlog — no de-hyping needed; "diamonds" is a documented culinary nickname/market fact, not an overclaim of anything contested. Thematically it rhymes with Ep. 7, "The Stinkhorn's Bargain": both are spore-dispersal-bargain stories — the stinkhorn hires flies with a corpse-smell above ground; the truffle hires mammals with an underground scent. A nice bestiary pairing.)*
**The big question / hook:** Almost every fungus in this bestiary grows up into the air and lets the world carry its spores off for free. The truffle did the opposite — it buried its fruiting body where no wind can reach it, and so it has to build a smell strong enough to hire an animal to dig it back up and eat it.

**Key points:**
1. A truffle is the subterranean (hypogeous) fruiting body of an ectomycorrhizal ascomycete — mainly genus *Tuber*, but 100+ other genera have independently taken up the same underground habit. The truffle is the reproductive tip of a much larger fungus in symbiosis with tree roots (oak, hazel, beech, birch, pine). [established]
2. Because it fruits underground, the truffle can't disperse spores by wind or water; nearly all truffles depend on mycophagous animals. Ripe truffles emit volatiles that attract vectors; chitin-walled spores survive the gut and disperse in droppings — analogous to seed dispersal in fruit. [established]
3. Aroma chemistry: largely absent when immature, intensifies as spores ripen. Base note = sulfur volatiles in all species (dimethyl sulfide/disulfide/trisulfide); white truffle *Tuber magnatum* is characterized by 2,4-dithiapentane (bis(methylthio)methane). [established]
4. Natural spore-carriers: deer, rodents (voles, squirrels, chipmunks), birds. Humans historically used pigs (sows); Italy banned pig truffle-hunting in 1985 (rooting damages the mycelium and drops yields); dogs now preferred — trainable to point not dig, and no appetite for the truffle. [established]
5. Culture/market beat: "diamonds of the kitchen," fresh truffles sell for thousands of dollars a pound on rarity + aroma; most commercial "truffle oil" contains no truffle and is flavored with synthetic 2,4-dithiapentane (a real, legal food additive — one loud note standing in for a whole chord). [established]
6. Honesty beat (the "Where we get honest" box): the famous androstenol pig-pheromone story. Claus, Hoppen & Karg (1981) found androstenol — a boar sex-pheromone steroid — in the black truffle and proposed it explains the sow's frantic digging. Perfumery folklore ever since. But Talou et al. (1990) found the compound truffle pigs *and* dogs actually localize is dimethyl sulfide, not androstenol; dogs (no boar-pheromone angle) find truffles fine. Hold the androstenol *presence* as established; hold the pheromone-*causation* story as contested/contradicted, not settled. [established presence / contested causation]
7. Frontier micro-note: some aroma compounds (certain thiophene derivatives) appear to be produced by bacteria living in the truffle, not the fungus itself (Splivallo et al. 2015); how much of the bouquet is microbial vs. fungal is still being worked out. [preliminary]

**Claims-risk flag:** none for §4 hard lines (no psilocybin, no cultivation instruction, no health/medical claim). Two items deliberately excluded at Content stage rather than hedged: (a) truffle "aphrodisiac / adaptogen" health-adjacent framing that appears in some popular sources — cut entirely to stay clear of the §4 #3 line, same discipline as CO-C-07's cancer-compound cut; (b) foraging/how-to-hunt instruction — the pig/dog history is included as culture, not as a how-to. The synthetic-truffle-oil beat is a factual consumer/culture note (what's in the bottle), not a health claim, and actually reinforces the brand's grounding credibility.
