**The big question / hook:** Why would a small dead caterpillar with a fungus growing out of its head become one of the most expensive substances on Earth — worth more than gold by weight? The biology is astonishing on its own; the price is built on something else entirely.

**Key points:**
1. Get-it-right / biology: it is a fungus, not a plant. *Ophiocordyceps sinensis* (an entomopathogen, relative of the CO-C-02 mind-snatcher) infects ghost-moth (Hepialidae) larvae living underground at 3,000–5,000 m on the Tibetan Plateau/Himalayas, kills and mummifies the caterpillar, then grows a stalk-like fruiting body (stroma) out of the dead larva's head that breaks the soil surface to release spores. "Winter worm, summer grass" (dōng chóng xià cǎo / yartsa gunbu) is a literal description of what's seen. Reclassified from *Cordyceps sinensis* → *Ophiocordyceps sinensis* in 2007. [established]
2. Trade/economics: "Himalayan gold." Top grade has traded above US$100,000/kg (a widely-reported 2017 Beijing figure put best material >$140,000/kg, >3× the price of gold by weight); ~900% price rise on the plateau 1998–2008; entirely hand-collected; the single largest cash-income source for many rural plateau households, a meaningful slice of local economies in Tibet/Qinghai/Sichuan/Yunnan/Nepal/Bhutan. [established]
3. Conservation/conflict: IUCN **Vulnerable**; treated as endangered in China; a 2018 *PNAS* study (Hopping, Chignell & Lambin) attributes decline to overharvesting + climate change, with falling yields; deadly disputes over collecting grounds (Nepal 2009 murders, convictions; role in Nepal's civil war); wild fruiting bodies can carry high arsenic/heavy metals → China regulated sale from 2016. [established]
4. The reputation (the point of the "trade hype" half): the entire value rests on a ~600-year-old reputation as a tonic/aphrodisiac — oldest written mention late 15th c. (Zurkhar Nyamnyi Dorje, Tibetan medicine), entered Chinese pharmacopeia 1694 (Wang Ang). Reported strictly as **folklore/marketing** — documented culture + commerce, NOT evidence of effect. The efficacy question is explicitly deferred to the grounding pillar (a future Series B / Theme D item, cf. D4 "Cordyceps and the Stamina Story"). Published assessments: "marketed for various health benefits but lacks sufficient scientific evidence for safety or effectiveness." [folklore/marketing]
5. Two grounding notes (Pillar-4 wink, established): (a) cordycepin, the compound the marketing leans on, is the signature of the cheaper cultivable cousin *Cordyceps militaris*; wild *O. sinensis* has little to none (a 2017 biosynthesis study concluded it makes none). (b) Almost nothing sold as "cordyceps" is the wild worm-and-grass: it's overwhelmingly grain-grown mycelium or substitute species (C. militaris, Samsoniella hepiali), because the full wild fruiting body resisted farming for decades. [established]

**Epistemic status (pre-flag):** four established beats (biology, economics, conservation, cordycepin/substitute-supplement reality); one folklore/marketing beat (the medicinal reputation) that is load-bearing and cannot be cut without gutting the assigned "trade hype" half of the story.

**Claims-risk flag:** **HEALTH-CLAIM ADJACENT — ESCALATE (§4 #3, §5).** Unlike CO-C-07 (cancer-compound tangent) and CO-C-08 (aphrodisiac/adaptogen tangent), where the health-adjacent material was a genuinely cuttable side-note, here the medicinal *reputation* is the economic engine of the whole topic and cannot be removed.
