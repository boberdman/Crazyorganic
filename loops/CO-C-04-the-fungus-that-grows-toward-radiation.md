## A · TOPIC BRIEF (Research → Content)

**Pillar:** 1 — The unexplored kingdom (wonder)
**Working title:** The Fungus That Grows Toward Radiation (backlog: "The Fungus That Eats Radiation")
**Big question / hook:** Black, melanized fungi don't just survive inside the wrecked Chernobyl reactor — they thrive there and grow *toward* the radiation. The wonder is real. The viral claim that they *eat* radiation ("radiosynthesis") is a genuine but unconfirmed hypothesis. This is a perfect Pillar-1 + honesty piece: the established marvel and the over-sold mechanism, cleanly separated.
**Key points:**
1. Melanin-rich "black fungi" colonize and flourish in the highly radioactive Chernobyl zone; ~2,000 strains / ~200 species / ~98 genera isolated over ~15 years; some grow into and decompose radioactive reactor graphite ("hot particles"). [established]
2. These fungi are highly radioresistant; melanin is protective — it physically absorbs radiation and scavenges radiation-generated free radicals. [established]
3. Radiotropism: hyphae grow directionally toward beta/gamma sources *even when carbon is removed* as a confounder (Zhdanova 2004) — but the study itself could not identify the mechanism. [established (phenomenon) / mechanism contested-unknown]
4. "Radiosynthesis" hypothesis (Dadachova 2007): three melanized fungi gained biomass faster under ~500× background radiation; irradiation changed melanin's electronic (ESR) properties and tripled-to-quadrupled a melanin-driven electron-transfer reaction → proposed that melanin harvests radiation energy like chlorophyll harvests light. [preliminary (the lab effects) / contested (the energy-harvesting interpretation)]
5. Honest core: "eats radiation" is NOT demonstrated. No energy-harvesting pathway / radiation-driven carbon fixation shown; fungi were fed normal nutrients, so the growth boost may be radioprotection or more-efficient nutrient use. Critics: energy deposited too low, media nutrients sufficient. Even proponents' reviews say "more evidence needed." [contested]
6. Footnote from orbit: a 30-day ISS test of a ~1.7 mm *Cladosporium sphaerospermum* lawn lowered radiation beneath it ~2.17±0.25% vs control, with ~21% growth advantage in space — a single small proof-of-concept for a self-growing radiation shield. [preliminary]
**Epistemic status:** **Established** spine (thriving in the zone, radioresistance, melanin shielding, radiotropism) + a clean **Contested/Preliminary** beat on the *mechanism* ("radiosynthesis" / "eats radiation"). Healthy counterweight: continues to exercise the non-established rungs while staying anchored by the established marvel — no drift into speculation.
**Sources:** Zhdanova et al. 2004 (*Mycol. Res.* — census, hot-particle decomposition, radiotropism, mechanism not identified); Dadachova et al. 2007 (*PLoS ONE* — growth under radiation, melanin ESR/electron-transfer, radiosynthesis hypothesis); Dadachova & Casadevall 2008 (*Curr. Opin. Microbiol.* — proponents' own review hedging "more evidence needed"); Walberg thesis, U. Wisconsin (critical re-analysis: not demonstrated); Averesch, Shunk & Kern 2022 (*Front. Microbiol.* — peer-reviewed ISS shielding result).
**Claims-risk flag:** NONE for §4 hard lines (no health/medical/legal/psychedelic content). Handling notes: (a) **never state radiosynthesis / "eats radiation" as fact** — fence it Contested. (b) keep **radioresistance/radiotropism (established)** distinct from **energy-harvesting (contested)**. (c) note radiotropism's mechanism is unidentified, not solved. (d) use the **peer-reviewed ISS figure (~2.17±0.25%)**, NOT the widely-circulated 2020 *preprint* numbers (2.42% / 4.34%). (e) melanin as armor = established; melanin as "solar panel" = contested.
