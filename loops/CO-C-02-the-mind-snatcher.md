## A · TOPIC BRIEF (Research → Content)

**Pillar:** 1 — The unexplored kingdom (wonder)
**Working title:** The Mind-Snatcher
**Big question / hook:** A fungus marches a living carpenter ant to a precise spot, locks its jaws onto a leaf, and erupts from its head — and the most surprising part is the one thing it leaves untouched: the brain.
**Key points:**
1. Behavioral sequence is stereotyped: infected *Camponotus* leaves the canopy colony, descends to understory, walks erratically with whole-body convulsions, then clamps mandibles onto an abaxial (underside) leaf vein ~25 cm above soil; dies; days later a stalk grows from the back of the head and releases spores. [established]
2. Biting is synchronized around solar noon in the studied Thai population; ants die clustered in dense "graveyards" (>~25 cadavers/m²). [established — note population-specificity]
3. The "death grip" is a lock-jaw caused by mandibular-muscle atrophy / sustained hyper-contraction, with fungal cells packed in the head; holds the ant after death. [established]
4. The twist: 2017 3D reconstruction shows the fungus networks around muscle fibres throughout the body but leaves the brain uninvaded — behavior control without entering the brain. [established]
5. HOW it controls behavior is unknown: candidate compounds + disrupted circadian-clock genes proposed, no single molecule confirmed. [preliminary / contested]
6. Get-it-right: *O. unilateralis* is a species complex (many host-specific species), reclassified from *Cordyceps*; real inspiration for *The Last of Us* but insect-specialized — does not infect humans. [established]
**Epistemic status:** mostly **established** (Pillar-1 wonder), with the mechanism explicitly **preliminary/contested** — a healthy non-established rung that improves epistemic balance after a run of all-Established Series A/C pieces.
**Sources:** Hughes et al. 2011 BMC Ecology (primary); Mangold et al. 2019 J Exp Biol (primary); Fredericksen et al. 2017 PNAS (primary); Will/de Bekker et al. 2023 Annu. Rev. Microbiol. (review); Evans, Elliot & Hughes 2011 PLoS ONE (primary).
**Claims-risk flag:** NONE for §4 hard lines (no health/medical/legal/psychedelic content). Handling notes: (a) don't overclaim "mind control" — the brain is spared; frame the "mind" word as metaphor. (b) Keep solar-noon synchrony tied to the studied population, not stated as universal. (c) Reassure on the human-pandemic fiction without sensationalizing.
**Target formats:** Newsletter + 3 X posts.

Days later a stalk grows out of its head and rains spores on the ants below. It did all this. Almost none of it was its idea. 🧵 — *Established*

2. *(the precision)* The creepy part is how *exact* it is. *Ophiocordyceps* marches the ant to a specific spot — underside of a leaf vein, ~25 cm up, in the studied population timed to around solar noon — where conditions are perfect for the fungus to fruit. Then it wrecks the ant's jaw muscles into a permanent vice. The "death grip." — *Established*

3. *(the twist + honesty)* You'd assume it hijacks the brain. It doesn't. A 2017 study reconstructed infected ants in 3D: the fungus webs around the *muscles* throughout the body but leaves the brain intact. A body driven without the mind that normally drives it. How? Still genuinely unknown — that part's unsolved science, not settled fact. (And yes — this inspired *The Last of Us*. It can't infect you.) — *Established / mechanism Preliminary*
