## Stage 1 — Topic Brief (Research)

**Pillar:** 1 — The unexplored kingdom (wonder)
**Working title:** Nature's Rain-Powered Cannons *(title adjusted from backlog's "Nature's Tiniest Cannons" — "cannons" kept for the wonder, "rain-powered" added so the headline doesn't imply the fungus fires itself; same de-hyping discipline as CO-C-01 and CO-C-04)*
**The big question / hook:** Bird's-nest fungi look like tiny nests full of eggs, but they don't launch anything themselves — a falling raindrop does, in a fraction of a second, aimed by a cup shaped with such precision that the spore packet flies about a meter and lassos the nearest twig.

**Key points:**
1. Bird's-nest fungi (family Nidulariaceae; 5 genera — *Crucibulum*, *Cyathus*, *Mycocalia*, *Nidula*, *Nidularia*) form miniature cup-shaped fruiting bodies containing lens-shaped spore packets called peridioles. [established]
2. Dispersal is a passive "splash cup": raindrops (most effective near the rim) eject peridioles at 1–5 m/s using less than 2% of the raindrop's kinetic energy, at a mean ejection angle of 67–73°, traveling up to ~1 m. Measured directly with high-speed video. [established]
3. Most peridioles trail a funicular cord tipped with a sticky hapteron; on contact with a twig it unspools and wraps around it (tetherball-style), braking and anchoring the peridiole. [established]
4. History/get-it-right beat: ~200 years of wrong guesses after Clusius's 1601 first description — Jean-Jacques Paulet proposed an internal spring mechanism in the 1790s; Brodie's splash-cup analysis (1951) and 2013 high-speed video settled it. [established, historical]
5. Taxonomy note: the five genera are partly distinguished by whether a funicular cord is present at all — *Nidularia* has none. [established]
6. Honesty flag: the tether's role in landing the peridiole where a browsing herbivore might eat it, completing the life cycle via gut passage, is described in sources as the plausible/likely function — not something directly filmed or tracked end-to-end. Flag for a hedge, not a plain "established" stamp. [preliminary/inferred]

**Sources to use:**
- Hassett, M.O., Fischer, M.W.F., Sugawara, Z.T., Stolze-Rybczynski, J., Money, N.P. (2013). "Splash and grab: Biomechanics of peridiole ejection and function of the funicular cord in bird's nest fungi." *Fungal Biology* 117(10): 708–714. PMID 24119409.
