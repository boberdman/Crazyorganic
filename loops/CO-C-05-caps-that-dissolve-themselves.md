## A · TOPIC BRIEF (Research → Content)

**Pillar:** 1 — The unexplored kingdom (wonder)
**Working title:** Caps That Dissolve Themselves
**Big question / hook:** A mushroom that manufactures its own digestive enzymes and uses them to dissolve its own body into black ink — on a schedule, on purpose — to get its spores into the wind.

**Key points:**
1. "Inky caps" deliquesce: gills autodigest into black spore-laden liquid within hours of maturing. [established]
2. Mechanism is a controlled biochemical program, not passive decay: chitinase/glucanase enzymes activate in a wave from the gill edge nearest the stem outward, gene-expression-confirmed. [established]
3. Standard explanation for *why*: continuously clearing spent gill tissue keeps every remaining strip freshly exposed to air rather than trapped between crowded gills — the field's working explanation, not something proven by a direct comparative-dispersal experiment. [established mechanism / honestly flagged evidence gap on the purpose-framing]
4. Taxonomic "get it right": most species called "inky cap" were reclassified out of genus *Coprinus* in 2001 (molecular study) into *Coprinopsis*, *Coprinellus*, *Parasola* (family Psathyrellaceae); true *Coprinus* (shaggy mane) stayed in Agaricaceae. [established]
5. Same folk name, different chemistry: *Coprinopsis atramentaria* ("tippler's bane") contains coprine (alcohol-intolerance toxin); shaggy mane (*Coprinus comatus*) doesn't share that chemistry and is a prized edible. [established] — descriptive toxicology, not foraging instruction.
6. Wonder/culture beat: deliquescence fluid historically boiled with clove oil into usable writing ink. [established as documented practice]

**Epistemic status:** predominantly established; point 3's purpose-framing flagged for honest hedging (see QA).
**Sources:** Wikipedia "Coprinus comatus"; Redhead et al. 2001, *Taxon* 50(1):203–241 (as cited therein); "Transcriptome analysis of genes associated with autolysis of Coprinus comatus," *Scientific Reports* (2022) / PMC8847592; Cornell Mushroom Blog, "The Dish on Deliquescence in Coprinus Species"; Wikipedia "Coprine"; Nowakowski et al. 2020, *Phytotherapy Research* 34(11):2932–2944 / PMC7754439 (as cited in Wikipedia); MushroomExpert.com, "Coprinoid Mushrooms: The Inky Caps."
**Claims-risk flag:** None / no §4 hard line. Handling note: keep the coprine/alcohol paragraph descriptive toxicology only — never foraging or edibility instruction.
**Target formats:** Newsletter + 3 X posts.

Its fellow "inky cap," the common ink cap, carries a compound that disables your body's ability to clear alcohol's toxic byproduct for days. Folk names lump species together that biology keeps very much apart. (Not foraging advice — get a real ID.) — *Established*
