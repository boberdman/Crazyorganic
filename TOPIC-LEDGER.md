**CO-C-09 detail —** *The Caterpillar That Becomes a Plant.* Series C — Bestiary Ep.9 (pipeline **A9**), *Ophiocordyceps sinensis* — the caterpillar fungus / "winter worm, summer grass" / yartsa gunbu / "Himalayan gold." reclassified *Cordyceps sinensis* → *Ophiocordyceps sinensis* in 2007 (same "get-it-right on taxonomy" move as CO-C-02/05); "Himalayan gold" — top grade has traded above US$100,000/kg (a 2017 Beijing figure >$140,000/kg, >3× the price of gold by weight), ~900% price rise 1998–2008, entirely hand-collected, the single largest cash-income source for many rural plateau households; IUCN **Vulnerable** and declining under combined overharvesting + climate change, with deadly conflict over collecting grounds (Nepal 2009 murders/convictions; a role in Nepal's civil war) and high arsenic/heavy-metal levels in wild fruiting bodies (China-regulated since 2016); **CO-C-08 detail —** *Underground Diamonds.* Series C — Bestiary Ep.8 (pipeline **A8**). Core claims: a truffle is the subterranean (hypogeous) fruiting body of an ectomycorrhizal ascomycete — mainly genus *Tuber*, with 100+ other genera independently converging on the underground habit — living in symbiosis with tree roots (oak, hazel, beech, birch, pine); the truffle is just the reproductive tip of a much larger soil fungus [**established**]; because it fruits underground it can't wind-/water-disperse, so nearly all truffles depend on mycophagous animals — ripe truffles emit volatiles that attract vectors, and chitin-walled spores survive gut passage and disperse in droppings, analogous to fruit/seed dispersal [**established**]; aroma is largely absent when immature and intensifies as spores ripen, built on sulfur volatiles present in all species (dimethyl sulfide/disulfide/trisulfide) with 2,4-dithiapentane (bis(methylthio)methane) characteristic of the white truffle *Tuber magnatum* [**established**]; natural spore-carriers include deer, rodents (voles, squirrels, chipmunks) and birds; humans historically used pigs/sows, banned in Italy since 1985 (rooting damages the mycelium and drops yields), now mostly replaced by dogs that point rather than dig and don't eat the truffle [**established**]; culture/market beat — "diamonds of the kitchen," fresh truffles selling for thousands of dollars a pound, and the mischievous grounding note that most commercial "truffle oil" contains no truffle and is flavored with a synthetic version of 2,4-dithiapentane (a real, legal food additive — one loud note standing in for a whole chord) [**established**]. **CO-C-07 detail —** *The Stinkhorn's Bargain.* Series C — Bestiary Ep.7 (pipeline **A7**). Title kept as written in the backlog — no de-hyping needed, since "bargain" doesn't overclaim anything contested. Core claims: stinkhorns (family Phallaceae, ~21 genera / 77 species, worldwide, concentrated in the tropics) trade wind-based spore dispersal for insect-based dispersal, paying a real metabolic cost to build an elaborate gleba odor rather than releasing cheap wind-borne spores [**established**]; the odor is chemically engineered convergent mimicry of both carrion (sulfur compounds — methanethiol, hydrogen sulfide, dimethyl sulfide, dimethyl trisulfide) and feces (phenol, indole, p-cresol), confirmed independently convergent with fly-pollinated "carrion flowers" by a 2010 study comparing the octopus stinkhorn (*Clathrus archeri*) against seven such flower species [**established**]; the odor's compound count changes across the fungus's life — quiet in the egg, dozens of compounds at maturity, fading as slime is carried off (Pudil 2014) [**established**]; the egg-to-mature transformation is a "reverse/cellular origami" — the entire structure, including cell wall material, is pre-packed and folded inside the egg, unfurling via water-absorption turgor pressure rather than new cell growth, measured at up to 5 mm/minute in *Phallus indusiatus* (Roper & Seminara 2019, *Annual Review of Fluid Mechanics* — fetched and read in full via subagent verbatim-quote extraction) [**established**]; a named Austrian field study (Schremmer 1963) documented blow-flies feeding on the slime and defecating spore-dense droppings shortly after leaving, while beetles visit but mostly eat fungal tissue rather than disperse spores [**established**]; get-it-right/diversity beat — the family's genera converge on the same chemical trick from very different body plans: *Phallus* (simple stalk+cap), *Clathrus* (latticed cage — lattice stinkhorn *C. ruber*, octopus stinkhorn *C. archeri*), *Aseroë* (red starburst disc, "sea anemone fungus"), *Mutinus* (slender dog stinkhorns) [**established**]; history/culture beat — Aldrovandi's 1560 description, Gerard's 1597 "pricke mushroom," Linnaeus's 1753 naming (*impudicus* = "shameless"), and Gwen Raverat's 1952 memoir describing her aunt Henrietta "Etty" Darwin (Charles Darwin's daughter) hunting and burning stinkhorns near Cambridge "because of the morals of the maids" [**established**, documented cultural anecdote].
